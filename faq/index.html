Florida law questions, answered.

The questions we get most often at consultations, by practice area, in plain English. If yours isn't here, ask during your free consultation. (Verified statute citations included; Angela still owns the legal-accuracy review.)

Family Law

Is Florida a no-fault divorce state?

+

Yes. Florida is a pure no-fault divorce state under Fla. Stat. § 61.052. To file, you only need to state the marriage is irretrievably broken (or that one spouse has been mentally incapacitated for at least three years). You do not need to prove wrongdoing by either spouse.

What is Florida Rule 12.285 mandatory disclosure?

+

Florida Family Law Rule 12.285 requires both parties in a divorce or support case to exchange specific financial documents within 45 days of service: tax returns, pay stubs, bank statements, retirement accounts, and similar records. Failure to comply can result in sanctions or evidentiary exclusions.

How is alimony calculated in Florida after the 2023 reform?

+

Florida's 2023 alimony reform (SB 1416) eliminated permanent alimony and introduced durational alimony with statutory caps tied to marriage length. Courts consider need and ability to pay along with the factors listed in Fla. Stat. § 61.08.

How does Florida decide child custody and time-sharing?

+

Custody is decided as time-sharing under Fla. Stat. § 61.13 using the best interests of the child standard. Florida law presumes that equal time-sharing is in the child's best interests unless rebutted by evidence on the statutory factors.

Are prenuptial agreements enforceable in Florida?

+

Yes, under the Florida Uniform Premarital Agreement Act (Fla. Stat. § 61.079), if the agreement is in writing, signed voluntarily, and based on fair financial disclosure (or a valid waiver). Postnuptial agreements are enforceable under similar contract-and-fairness principles.

Can I modify a Florida child support or custody order?

+

Yes — but only on a substantial, material, and unanticipated change in circumstances. Modification requires a new petition and court order.

What happens if my ex won't pay child support?

+

Florida courts can enforce support through contempt proceedings, wage garnishment, license suspension, and tax refund interception. Filing a motion for contempt under Fla. Stat. § 61.14 is the typical first step.

Can a relative get temporary custody?

+

Yes. Florida's Temporary Custody by Extended Family statute (Fla. Stat. ch. 751) allows a qualifying relative to petition for temporary legal custody when both parents consent or are unable or unwilling to care for the child.

How does adoption work in Florida?

+

Florida adoption is governed by Fla. Stat. ch. 63. The process generally requires consent (or termination of parental rights), a home study, an adoption petition, and a final hearing where the court enters a Final Judgment of Adoption.

Can I relocate with my child after divorce?

+

Florida's relocation statute (Fla. Stat. § 61.13001) requires written consent of every other parent or court approval before any move of more than 50 miles for 60 consecutive days or more.

Can attorney's fees be awarded against the other party?

+

Yes. Fla. Stat. § 61.16 allows the court to award attorney's fees and costs based on the parties' need and ability to pay.

Juvenile Dependency & Termination of Parental Rights

What is a shelter hearing?

+

A shelter hearing is the first court hearing in a Florida dependency case after a child is removed from their home. Under Fla. Stat. § 39.402, it generally occurs within 24 hours of removal.

What is a case plan?

+

The written document, governed by Fla. Stat. § 39.6011, that lists tasks, services, and timelines a parent must complete to be reunified with the child.

How long do I have to reunify?

+

Florida's permanency goal is generally reunification within 12 months of removal.

When can the State terminate parental rights?

+

The grounds are listed in Fla. Stat. § 39.806. They include voluntary surrender, abandonment, egregious conduct, chronic substance abuse, and failure to substantially comply with the case plan. Grounds must be proven by clear and convincing evidence.

What does the Guardian ad Litem do?

+

The GAL is appointed under Fla. Stat. § 39.820 to represent the best interests of the child. The GAL investigates, attends hearings, makes recommendations, and is independent of both parents and DCF.

Will my child be placed with relatives first?

+

Yes — Florida's dependency law gives a strong preference to placing a removed child with a fit and willing relative or a non-relative with a close relationship before licensed foster care.

Safety Plan vs Case Plan?

+

A Safety Plan is a short-term, often pre-shelter agreement to keep a child safe. A Case Plan is the formal court-filed plan with reunification tasks.

Do siblings have to be placed together?

+

Florida requires reasonable efforts to place siblings together when removed, with frequent visitation when separation is unavoidable, under Fla. Stat. § 39.4085.

Do I have a right to a lawyer?

+

Indigent parents have a right to court-appointed counsel in Florida dependency cases. Even when not indigent, retaining private counsel is strongly recommended.

Can I appeal a TPR order?

+

Yes. Notice of appeal must be filed within 30 days of the order; deadlines in dependency appeals are strictly enforced.

Estate Planning & Probate

Do I need a will in Florida?

+

If you die without a will, your assets pass under the intestate succession statute (Fla. Stat. ch. 732), which may not match your wishes.

Florida's will execution requirements?

+

Under Fla. Stat. § 732.502, a Florida will must be in writing, signed by the testator at the end, and signed by two attesting witnesses in the presence of the testator and of each other.

Revocable vs irrevocable trust?

+

A revocable trust can be amended or revoked during the grantor's life and is commonly used for probate avoidance. An irrevocable trust generally cannot be changed once funded; it is used for asset protection, tax planning, and Medicaid planning.

How does Florida homestead protection work?

+

Florida's constitutional homestead protection shields the primary residence from most creditor claims, caps property tax increases (Save Our Homes), and limits devise when survived by a spouse or minor child.

Formal vs summary administration?

+

Formal administration is Florida's standard probate. Summary administration (Fla. Stat. ch. 735) is available for estates of $75,000 or less in non-exempt assets, or where the decedent has been deceased more than two years.

How long does Florida probate take?

+

Summary: a few weeks to a few months. Formal: typically six to twelve months because of the mandatory three-month creditor claim period.

What is a Lady Bird deed?

+

An enhanced life estate deed that lets the owner keep full lifetime control and pass the property at death without probate.

Do I need a healthcare surrogate?

+

Yes — most Florida adults should have a designation under Fla. Stat. ch. 765, paired with a living will.

What does a durable POA do?

+

Under Fla. Stat. ch. 709, a durable POA lets a person you trust handle financial and legal matters for you. Florida no longer recognizes "springing" POAs signed after October 1, 2011.

Does Florida have a state estate tax?

+

No. Florida has no state estate tax and no inheritance tax. Federal estate tax still applies above the federal exemption.

What is the federal estate tax exemption?

+

For 2026, the exemption is approximately $14 million per individual; confirm against the current IRS revenue procedure for the year of death or gift.

What is business succession planning?

+

Preparing the transfer of ownership and management of a closely held business — through buy-sell agreements, life-insurance funding, trusts, or family transitions.

Civil Litigation

Do I need to send a demand letter before suing?

+

It depends on the claim. Some Florida statutes require pre-suit notice — Fla. Stat. ch. 558 (construction defect), § 768.28 (claims against the State), and certain insurance and FDUTPA claims.

Statute of limitations for breach of contract?

+

Under Fla. Stat. § 95.11, written contracts: five years; oral contracts: four years.

How long to sue for property damage?

+

Four years under Fla. Stat. § 95.11. Different limitations apply to construction defects and claims against governmental entities.

How does construction litigation differ?

+

Florida's Chapter 558 imposes a mandatory pre-suit notice and opportunity-to-cure process. Construction claims also intersect with lien rights under Fla. Stat. ch. 713.

Can I recover attorney's fees?

+

Florida follows the "American Rule" unless a contract or statute provides otherwise. A proposal for settlement under § 768.79 can shift fees.

Florida's summary judgment standard?

+

Effective May 1, 2021, Florida adopted the federal summary judgment standard (Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.510).

Can I demand a jury trial?

+

Yes for most legal claims. A jury demand must be made in writing under Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.430 or it is waived.

Is mediation required before trial?

+

Most Florida civil divisions require it under Fla. Stat. ch. 44 and local case-management orders.

How long to appeal a civil final judgment?

+

Notice of appeal must be filed within 30 days of rendition of the final order under Fla. R. App. P. 9.110.
